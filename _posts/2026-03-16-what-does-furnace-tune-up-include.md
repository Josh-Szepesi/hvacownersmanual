Most homeowners schedule a furnace tune-up because their utility bill crept up or someone told them to. But if you ask what actually happens during that appointment, you'll usually get a shrug. I've talked to enough HVAC technicians and homeowners to know that the tune-up process varies widely between companies - and understanding what you're paying for helps you ask the right questions and spot the ones cutting corners.

**Safety Note:** Furnace work involves natural gas, combustion byproducts, and electrical components. Before any DIY inspection, turn off the furnace at the thermostat and switch the power off at the breaker. If you ever smell gas near the furnace, do not touch any switches - leave the house and call your gas utility immediately. A licensed technician should handle any repairs involving the gas valve, heat exchanger, or flue system. I strongly recommend keeping a working CO detector within 10 feet of your furnace at all times - carbon monoxide from a cracked heat exchanger can be fatal before you notice symptoms.

## What a Furnace Tune-Up Is (and Is Not)

A tune-up is a preventive maintenance visit, not a repair call. Its purpose is to catch problems before they become failures, verify the furnace is operating safely, and confirm the system is running at close to its rated efficiency. It is not a parts replacement service unless the technician finds something worn or failing - at which point they should give you a separate estimate.

I have found that the best technicians treat a tune-up as a diagnostic inspection with cleaning included. The worst ones show up, swap your filter, and hand you a bill. Knowing what a thorough visit looks like helps you hold any company accountable.

## The Typical Furnace Tune-Up Checklist

### Filter Inspection and Replacement

The technician should check your air filter and either replace it or note its condition. Some companies include a standard 1-inch filter in the service price; others charge extra. In my experience, most tune-up packages include a basic fiberglass filter, but if you use a [high-MERV filter like a MERV 11 or 13](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=MERV+13+furnace+filter&tag=mbbeekeeping-20), you'll want to supply your own or confirm the tech carries the right size.

Filter replacement takes 5 minutes. If that's all the tech does during a "tune-up," ask for an itemized explanation of everything else.

### Burner Inspection and Cleaning

Gas burners accumulate scale, rust, and carbon deposits over a heating season. A technician should visually inspect each burner port for blockages and clean them if needed. They'll also check the burner flame - a clean, steady blue flame is what you want. Yellow, flickering, or rolling flames suggest incomplete combustion, which wastes fuel and produces more CO.

This is one of the steps where skimping costs you efficiency. Dirty burners can drop furnace efficiency by 10% or more over a season.

### Heat Exchanger Visual Inspection

The heat exchanger is the most critical safety component in a gas furnace. It separates combustion gases from the air circulating into your home. Cracks or holes in the heat exchanger allow carbon monoxide to enter your living space - this is the number one reason annual tune-ups matter.

A proper inspection involves removing the burner assembly, using a flashlight and mirror to look for cracks, rust, or distortion, and sometimes inserting a camera into the flue passages. Some techs use a combustion analyzer to detect exhaust gas traces in the supply air stream, which is the most reliable method for catching small cracks. If a technician only glances at the heat exchanger from the front panel and calls it good, that's not a thorough inspection.

If a crack is confirmed, the furnace should be shut down immediately. A cracked heat exchanger is not a repair most homeowners should delay or defer.

### Flue and Venting Check

The technician should check the flue pipe for blockages, corrosion, and proper slope. Birds, debris, and condensation buildup can all partially obstruct the flue. They should also verify the draft - the pressure differential that pulls combustion gases out of the furnace and up the flue. Poor draft means CO can back-draft into the home.

For high-efficiency (condensing) furnaces with PVC intake and exhaust pipes, the tech should check the terminations outside for obstructions and inspect the condensate drain line for clogs.

### Blower Motor and Belt Inspection

The blower motor pushes conditioned air through your ducts. The technician should measure the motor's amperage draw and compare it to the nameplate rating - a motor drawing significantly more than its rated amperage is working too hard and approaching failure.

On older furnaces with belt-driven blowers, the belt condition and tension should be checked. Modern direct-drive motors don't have belts, but the capacitor - which helps the motor start - should still be tested. A weak capacitor causes hard starts and shortens motor life.

If you've been noticing [uneven heating between rooms](https://hvacownersmanual.com/2026/03/14/fix-uneven-heating-cooling-between-rooms.html), a struggling blower motor is one of the first things I'd check.

### Igniter and Flame Sensor

Most modern furnaces use a hot surface igniter (a fragile silicon carbide or silicon nitride element) to light the burner. The tech should inspect the igniter for cracks and measure its resistance. A weak igniter leads to delayed ignition or no-heat calls in the middle of winter.

The flame sensor is a small rod that confirms the burner is lit before the gas valve stays open. It gets coated with oxidation over time and needs light cleaning with steel wool or fine emery cloth. A dirty flame sensor is one of the most common causes of [furnaces that short-cycle](https://hvacownersmanual.com/2026/03/11/why-furnace-short-cycles.html) - the burner lights, the sensor doesn't confirm the flame, and the furnace shuts off after a few seconds.

### Thermostat Calibration and Controls Check

The technician should verify that the thermostat is calling correctly and that the furnace is responding at the right temperature differential. They should also run through the control board's sequence of operation - making sure the draft inducer, igniter, gas valve, and blower all come on in the right order and within spec.

This step catches intermittent control board failures that wouldn't show up during a normal call but will eventually strand you without heat.

### Gas Pressure Measurement

Proper manifold gas pressure is critical for safe and efficient combustion. A technician should measure both the incoming supply pressure and the manifold pressure after the gas valve. Values outside the manufacturer's spec (usually printed on the rating plate) indicate a problem with the gas valve, regulator, or supply line.

Low gas pressure starves the burner; high pressure causes rough ignition and excessive heat. Neither is safe or efficient.

### Combustion Analysis

This is the step that separates thorough technicians from quick-visit ones. A combustion analyzer - inserted into the flue - measures O2, CO2, CO, and stack temperature to calculate real-world combustion efficiency. A well-tuned gas furnace should show CO in the stack under 100 ppm (parts per million). Higher readings point to incomplete combustion, which means wasted fuel and potential safety risk.

In my experience, fewer than half of tune-up technicians in residential HVAC carry or use a combustion analyzer. If efficiency and safety matter to you, ask before booking whether combustion analysis is included.

## What a Tune-Up Costs

A standard residential furnace tune-up typically runs $80-$150 from an independent HVAC contractor. National service companies often charge $100-$200 but may run frequent discount promotions at $49-$79 that get a tech in the door to upsell additional services.

Be skeptical of extremely low prices - a $49 tune-up often means a filter change and a visual look around. Expect to pay in the $100-$130 range for a visit that includes everything on the checklist above.

Some contractors offer annual maintenance agreements that cover both the furnace (fall) and AC (spring) for a flat fee, usually $150-$250 per year. If your equipment is more than 5 years old, I find these plans worth the price for the peace of mind alone.

## Is a Furnace Tune-Up Worth It?

I'd answer yes for most homeowners, with one condition: you need to verify the company you hire actually performs a thorough inspection. A real tune-up extends equipment life, maintains efficiency, and - most importantly - catches heat exchanger cracks before they become a CO hazard.

The DOE's Energy Star program recommends annual maintenance for gas furnaces, and it's a requirement for keeping most manufacturer warranties valid. Skipping tune-ups to save $100 and then facing a $1,200 emergency service call in January is a trade I've seen homeowners regret.

If your furnace is under 5 years old and you maintain the filter regularly, you can sometimes go 2 years between tune-ups without consequence. Older furnaces - especially those over 15 years old - benefit from annual attention.

For more on maintaining your system and understanding efficiency options, the [U.S. Department of Energy's guide to furnaces and boilers](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/furnaces-and-boilers) is worth reading before you schedule a service call.

## FAQ

**How long does a furnace tune-up take?**

A thorough tune-up should take 45 minutes to 1.5 hours depending on the furnace age, condition, and whether the technician finds anything needing attention. If a tech is in and out in under 30 minutes, ask specifically what they checked and cleaned.

**Can I do a furnace tune-up myself?**

Homeowners can handle a few things: replacing the air filter, cleaning the flame sensor, and visually inspecting the area around the furnace. But gas pressure measurement, heat exchanger inspection, combustion analysis, and anything involving the gas train should be left to a licensed HVAC technician. The risk isn't worth the savings.

**How often should I get a furnace tune-up?**

Once a year, ideally in early fall before the heating season. If you have an older furnace (15+ years) or one that's had repair history, stick to annual service without fail. Newer furnaces in good condition can sometimes go two seasons, but annual is still the standard recommendation.

**What's the difference between a tune-up and a furnace cleaning?**

A cleaning typically refers to the burner and blower cleaning tasks within a tune-up. The terms are sometimes used interchangeably, but a full tune-up includes safety checks, measurements, and operational testing that go well beyond just cleaning components. When booking, ask specifically what's included.

**Should I replace the filter before the technician arrives?**

No - let the technician see the current filter so they can assess how quickly your system loads up and whether you need a different filter type or replacement frequency. A clogged filter tells the tech something about how the system has been running.
